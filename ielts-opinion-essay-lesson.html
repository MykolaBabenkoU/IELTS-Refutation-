Step 1

What is a counter argument?

Before writing one, understand exactly what it is and why examiners reward it.

A counter argument is an opposing viewpoint that challenges your main argument. It presents evidence or opinions that contrast with your thesis, and is always followed by a refutation — a response that explains why the opposing view is flawed or less convincing.

Part 1 — Counter argument
The opposing viewpoint, stated fairly. You are giving the other side a genuine chance to speak.
Part 2 — Refutation
A direct response explaining why the opposing view is flawed, limited, or outweighed by your argument.

Together, these two parts form a single paragraph that shows the examiner you can evaluate multiple perspectives logically — one of the clearest signals of Band 7+ critical thinking.

🧠
Critical thinking
Discussing both sides shows you can evaluate perspectives logically, not just list opinions.
💪
More persuasive
Refuting the opposite side makes your argument stronger and more objective to the reader.
✍️
Deeper writing
Instead of sounding one-sided, your essay becomes more dynamic and interesting to read.
Step 2

Essay structure

A standard agree/disagree essay has four parts. Body paragraph 2 is where critical thinking becomes visible to the examiner.

1
Introduction
Paraphrase the topic → clear Thesis Statement (your position in one sentence)
2
Body paragraph 1 — Your main argument
Topic sentence → Evidence or example → Explanation of why it matters
3
Body paragraph 2 — Counter argument + Refutation ★
Acknowledge the opposing view fairly → counter it with a stronger, specific argument. This is the key paragraph for Band 7+.
4
Conclusion
Restate your thesis in new words + brief summary. No new information.
✓ Use a counter argument when
You have a clear position on one side
You can identify a real flaw in the opposing view
The topic has two genuinely distinct sides
You want to demonstrate critical thinking
✗ Avoid it when
Your position is unclear or shifting
You cannot find a specific weakness to refute
It makes your essay lose coherence
The question asks only to "discuss both views"
Step 3

Annotated examples

Notice how the counter argument is fair, and how the refutation introduces a specific, concrete weakness — never just "I disagree".

Pivot word
Refutation
Example 1 — Social media and teenagers
PartContent
Main argumentExcessive use of social media negatively affects teenagers' health, studies, and social relationships.
Counter argumentSome people argue that social media is useful for connection and knowledge sharing.
RefutationHowever, overuse can harm real-life relationships, reduce critical thinking due to unreliable content, and negatively impact daily life.

Some people argue that social media is useful for connection and knowledge sharing. However, this argument fails to consider that overuse actively harms real-life relationships, erodes critical thinking through exposure to unreliable content, and disrupts the daily routines of adolescents at a critical stage of development.

The refutation adds three specific harms — not a vague claim. Specificity is what distinguishes Band 7 from Band 5.

Example 2 — Environment vs economy

Critics might argue that economic growth is necessary to fund environmental projects and that slowing development could hurt global progress. While this point has some validity, it does not account for the fact that unchecked industrial expansion causes the very environmental damage it claims to fund a solution for — a contradiction that undermines the argument entirely and highlights the need to prioritise sustainability from the outset.

This refutation exposes an internal contradiction in the opposing view — a sophisticated move that signals strong analytical thinking.

Example 3 — Video games (practice topic)

The opponents might argue that video games can improve cognitive skills, such as problem-solving and hand-eye coordination. While it is true that some games offer these benefits, this argument does not account for the reality that excessive gaming typically leads to a sedentary lifestyle, chronic sleep disruption, and a measurable decline in academic performance — impacts that far outweigh any cognitive gains for the majority of teenage players.

"Does not account for the reality that" is a clean academic pivot. It accepts the grain of truth while redirecting to a bigger, more important problem.

Phrase bank

Use these to signal each move clearly. Examiners read fast — make your structure obvious through language choices.

It is true that …

However, this argument fails to consider …
Nevertheless, …

Weighing and explaining
… is largely outweighed by …
… does not account for …
… is a short-sighted view because …
While this point is partly valid, it does not account for …
… pales in comparison to …
Phrases to avoid in Task 2
✗But …— too informal; never start a sentence with it
✗Although I agree, I disagree …— contradictory and confusing
✗In my humble opinion …— cliché that lowers your Band Score
✗On one hand … on other hand …— missing "the"; also blurs your position
✗Both sides have their pros and cons …— vague; fails to commit to a position
✗It is bad / harmful / wrong …— too vague; always name the specific mechanism or consequence
